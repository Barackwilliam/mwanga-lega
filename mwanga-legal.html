How to Register a Company in Tanzania: A Complete 2026 Guide

DM
Adv. Daniel Mushi — Managing Partner
Corporate & Commercial Law · Mwanga Legal Associates

Starting a business in Tanzania is an exciting decision — but navigating the legal registration process can be overwhelming without the right guidance. At Mwanga Legal Associates, we have assisted hundreds of Tanzanian entrepreneurs and international investors register their businesses. This comprehensive guide walks you through every step of the process in 2026.

Step 1: Choose Your Business Structure

Before registering, you must decide what type of business entity best suits your goals. In Tanzania, the most common structures include:

  • Private Limited Company (Ltd) — Most popular for SMEs and foreign investors. Requires a minimum of 1 shareholder and 1 director.
  • Sole Proprietorship — Simplest form for individual traders. Full personal liability applies.
  • Partnership — Two or more individuals sharing profits and liabilities under a partnership deed.
  • Branch Office — Foreign companies can establish a branch in Tanzania without incorporating a separate local entity.
  • Public Limited Company (PLC) — For larger entities intending to issue shares to the public or list on the Dar es Salaam Stock Exchange (DSE).
"The choice of business structure has long-term implications for taxation, liability, and fundraising capacity. Always consult a legal professional before deciding." — Adv. Daniel Mushi

Step 2: Name Search and Reservation at BRELA

The Business Registrations and Licensing Agency (BRELA) is the government authority responsible for business registration in Tanzania. Your first step is to conduct a name search to ensure your proposed company name is unique and not already registered.

You can conduct name searches digitally through the BRELA Online Registration System (ORS) at ors.brela.go.tz. Once approved, your name is reserved for 30 days during which you must complete registration.

  • Name must not be identical or similar to an existing registered company
  • Must not include words like "National", "Tanzania", or "Government" without special approval from the relevant authority
  • Must end with "Limited" or "Ltd" for private limited companies

Step 3: Prepare and Submit Incorporation Documents

Once your name is reserved, prepare the following documents for submission to BRELA:

  • Memorandum of Association (MoA) — Defines the company's objectives, powers, and scope of business
  • Articles of Association (AoA) — Internal rules governing management of the company
  • Form 14A — Statement of nominal capital and first directors
  • Form 14B — Consent of first directors to act in their capacity
  • Certified copies of all directors' and shareholders' national IDs or passports
  • Power of Attorney — If filing through a legal representative (highly recommended)

Step 4: Obtain a Certificate of Incorporation

Upon successful review of your documents, BRELA issues a Certificate of Incorporation. This is your company's legal birth certificate — it confirms the existence of your business under the Companies Act (Cap. 212 R.E. 2002) of Tanzania.

The process typically takes 3 to 7 working days when using the digital ORS portal. At Mwanga Legal Associates, we regularly complete incorporations within 2 working days on behalf of our clients.

Step 5: Register for Tax with Tanzania Revenue Authority (TRA)

Every registered company in Tanzania must obtain a Taxpayer Identification Number (TIN) from the Tanzania Revenue Authority. Registration is done at tra.go.tz or at your nearest TRA office across the country.

  • TIN registration is free and mandatory for all businesses
  • VAT registration is required if annual turnover exceeds TZS 100 million
  • You may also need to register for Skills Development Levy (SDL) and NSSF contributions for employees

Step 6: Obtain a Business Licence

Depending on your type of business, you may need a general business licence from the local government authority (LGA) in your area, as well as sector-specific licences from regulatory bodies such as TFDA (food and drugs), SUMATRA (transport), TCAA (aviation), or the Bank of Tanzania (financial services).

Step 7: Open a Corporate Bank Account

You will need your Certificate of Incorporation, TIN certificate, and a board resolution to open a corporate bank account. Most Tanzanian banks including CRDB, NMB, Stanbic, Absa, and NBC offer dedicated business accounts with competitive features tailored to different sectors.

What Does It Cost?

Government fees at BRELA start from approximately TZS 70,000 for a private limited company. Our legal fees for a complete registration package — including name search, document preparation, filing, TIN registration, and liaison with BRELA — start from TZS 850,000 with no hidden charges.

Understanding Land Ownership Laws in Tanzania: What Every Buyer Must Know

GM
Adv. Grace Mwakyusa — Property & Land Law Expert
Real Estate & Land Law · Mwanga Legal Associates

Tanzania's land tenure system is one of the most complex and frequently misunderstood aspects of doing business or investing in the country. Whether you are a Tanzanian citizen, a member of the diaspora, or a foreign investor, understanding how land ownership works in Tanzania is essential before any property transaction.

The Foundation: All Land Belongs to the State

Under the Land Act (Cap. 113) and the Village Land Act (Cap. 114) of Tanzania, all land in Tanzania is public land vested in the President of the United Republic of Tanzania as trustee on behalf of all citizens. No individual or corporation actually "owns" land in Tanzania in the absolute sense. Instead, they hold rights of occupancy over the land for a specified period.

"Understanding the difference between a Right of Occupancy and a Certificate of Title is the most important lesson for any property buyer in Tanzania." — Adv. Grace Mwakyusa

The Three Categories of Land in Tanzania

1. General Land

This is land that is not village land or reserved land. Urban land, peri-urban areas, and most commercial land falls under this category. It is managed by the Commissioner for Lands at the Ministry of Lands. Rights over general land are held through a Right of Occupancy (RoO) granted by the government.

2. Village Land

Approximately 70% of Tanzania's land area is village land. It is managed by Village Councils under the Village Land Act. Citizens living in villages may hold a Customary Right of Occupancy (CCRO), which is now a recognised, bankable form of land tenure that can be used as collateral for bank loans at institutions such as CRDB and NMB.

3. Reserved Land

This includes national parks, game reserves, forest reserves, and government buildings. No private rights of occupancy can be granted over reserved land under any circumstances.

What is a Right of Occupancy (RoO)?

A Granted Right of Occupancy is the highest form of land tenure for general land in Tanzania. It is granted by the Commissioner for Lands for a period of 33 years, 66 years, or 99 years, and is renewable. The holder receives a Certificate of Right of Occupancy (CRO) — commonly known as a "title deed".

  • RoOs can be transferred, mortgaged, or subdivided subject to government approval
  • Annual land rent must be paid to the government; failure to pay can result in revocation
  • Development conditions must be met within the stipulated period specified in the certificate

Can Foreigners Own Land in Tanzania?

Under the Land Act, non-citizens cannot hold a Granted Right of Occupancy in Tanzania. However, foreign investors can hold land through a Derivative Right of Occupancy granted by a Tanzanian-registered company — or through the Tanzania Investment Centre (TIC) framework, which allows foreign investors to be granted a Right of Occupancy for investment purposes only.

This means foreign investors typically establish a Tanzanian-registered company (which can be 100% foreign-owned in most sectors) and the company holds the land. The company must be registered with TIC and hold a valid TIC certificate of incentives.

Key Risks to Watch Out For When Buying Land

  • Double allocation: The same plot allocated to two different parties. Always verify at the Lands Registry before paying any deposit.
  • Encumbrances: Mortgages, caveats, or court orders registered against the title that could block transfer.
  • Boundary disputes: Obtain an official survey plan from a licensed surveyor. Boundary conflicts are extremely common in peri-urban Tanzania.
  • Village land misdescribed as general land: Very common in peri-urban areas like Kibaha, Bagamoyo, Kunduchi, and Mbezi Beach in Dar es Salaam.
  • Unregistered sellers: Always confirm that the seller is the actual registered holder through an official search at the Lands Registry.

Legal Due Diligence Before Buying Land in Tanzania

Before signing any sale agreement or paying any deposit, thorough legal due diligence is essential. At Mwanga Legal Associates, our land law due diligence process includes:

  • Official search at the Lands Registry to confirm registered owner and existing encumbrances
  • Physical site visit and boundary confirmation with a licensed government surveyor
  • Verification of local authority planning approvals and zoning compliance with the City/Municipal Council
  • Review of any existing tenancies, occupants, or caretakers on the land
  • Confirmation of paid-up land rent with the Commissioner for Lands

Legal Requirements for Foreign Investors in Tanzania: 2026 Updated Framework

IS
Adv. Ibrahim Selemani — Litigation & Investment Specialist
Corporate & Investment Law · Mwanga Legal Associates

Tanzania continues to be one of East Africa's most attractive destinations for foreign direct investment. With abundant natural resources, a fast-growing middle class, a strategic position on the Indian Ocean coastline, and a government committed to economic reform under Vision 2025, the opportunities are significant. However, navigating the legal and regulatory framework requires careful preparation and the right legal partner.

The Tanzania Investment Act (Cap. 38)

The primary legislation governing foreign investment in Tanzania is the Tanzania Investment Act (Cap. 38). This Act establishes the Tanzania Investment Centre (TIC) as the one-stop-shop for foreign investors and sets out the rights, protections, and incentives available to registered investors operating in Tanzania.

In 2024–2025, the government introduced several reforms aimed at simplifying the investment process, including the integration of TIC's online portal with BRELA's ORS system and reduction of processing timelines for investment certificates to 10 working days.

Minimum Capital Requirements

To qualify for a TIC certificate and associated investment incentives, foreign investors must meet minimum capital thresholds:

  • Foreign-owned companies: Minimum investment of USD 500,000
  • Joint ventures (Tanzanian & foreign partners): Minimum investment of USD 100,000
  • Strategic sectors (tourism, agriculture, manufacturing): May qualify for reduced thresholds on a case-by-case basis approved by the TIC Board
"The TIC Certificate is not just a formality — it unlocks critical protections including guarantees against expropriation, the right to repatriate profits, and access to Special Economic Zone benefits." — Adv. Ibrahim Selemani

Restricted and Strategic Sectors

Not all business sectors are open to 100% foreign ownership. The following sectors require special approval or mandatory local equity participation:

  • Broadcasting and telecommunications: Minimum 25% local shareholding required under TCRA regulations
  • Mining and extractive industries: Governed separately by the Mining Act 2010 and regulated by TMAA with mandatory government equity
  • Banking and financial services: Subject to Bank of Tanzania licensing with specific capitalisation requirements
  • Insurance: Regulated by the Tanzania Insurance Regulatory Authority (TIRA) with local presence requirements
  • Legal services: Must be provided by advocates admitted to the Tanzanian bar under TLS supervision

Work Permits for Foreign Nationals in Tanzania

Every foreign national working in Tanzania must hold a valid work permit issued by the Immigration Services Department. The main categories relevant to investors include:

  • Class C Work Permit — For employed foreign nationals with a specific Tanzanian employer
  • Class B Self-Employment Permit — For business owners and investors operating their own enterprise in Tanzania
  • Residence Permit Class A — For investors who have made a qualifying investment registered with TIC

Work permits must be renewed annually and are linked to the validity of the employment contract or business licence. Violations carry significant penalties including heavy fines, deportation, and blacklisting from future applications.

Tax Incentives for TIC-Registered Investors

Foreign investors who register with TIC benefit from a range of tax incentives under the Income Tax Act and the TRA regulations, including:

  • Import duty exemptions on capital goods and raw materials for qualifying industries
  • Corporate tax holidays for qualifying projects in Export Processing Zones (EPZs) and Special Economic Zones (SEZs)
  • VAT deferment on imported capital equipment during the project development phase
  • Double Tax Agreements (DTAs) with over 12 countries including South Africa, India, Canada, Denmark, Italy, and Norway

Protecting Your Investment: Legal Safeguards

Tanzania is a signatory to the International Centre for Settlement of Investment Disputes (ICSID) and the Multilateral Investment Guarantee Agency (MIGA). This means foreign investors have access to international arbitration in the event of a dispute with the Tanzanian government — a powerful legal protection.

  • Protection against nationalisation or expropriation without fair, adequate, and prompt compensation
  • Right to repatriate profits, dividends, and capital after all tax obligations are met with the TRA
  • Access to the Tanzania Centre for Arbitration (TCAA) for commercial disputes with Tanzanian parties
  • Investment guarantees through bilateral investment treaties (BITs) signed with multiple countries

Practical Steps to Establish Your Business in Tanzania

  • Register a company at BRELA (private limited company recommended for most foreign investors)
  • Apply for a TIC Certificate of Incentives at the Tanzania Investment Centre in Dar es Salaam
  • Obtain TIN and VAT registration from the Tanzania Revenue Authority (TRA)
  • Apply for relevant sector licences from TFDA, SUMATRA, TCRA, BoT, or other sector regulators
  • Obtain work permits and residence permits for all expatriate staff through Immigration Services
  • Open a corporate bank account in Tanzania (CRDB, NMB, Stanbic, or Absa recommended)
  • Secure land or premises through a legally verified lease or Right of Occupancy from the Lands Registry
